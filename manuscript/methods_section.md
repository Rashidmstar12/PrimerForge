# Section: Computational Methods

To evaluate the predictive performance of PrimerForge, we curated a robust empirical dataset of 292 primer pairs. This dataset integrates experimentally validated positive primers from PrimerBank (Untergasser et al., 2012) and the live_ultra_empirical_db qPCR databases targeting diverse genes across human, mouse, and pathogen templates (including SARS-CoV-2 ARTIC/CDC panels and Influenza A/B CDC panels). The negative controls (n = 140) were programmatically designed to model realistic, borderline failure modes rather than trivial thermodynamic extremes. These include borderline melting temperature mismatches (Tm difference of 3.0–8.0°C), borderline self-hairpin free energies (hairpin dG of -4.5 to -6.0 kcal/mol), and borderline cross-hybridization (dimer dG of -5.5 to -7.0 kcal/mol). Additionally, we simulated non-thermodynamic experimental failures by incorporating sequence-derived faults (homopolymer runs greater than 4 nucleotides or 3'-terminal T/A dinucleotides) despite otherwise optimal biophysical scores.

For each primer pair, we extracted 36 biophysical and sequence-derived features. Melting temperatures (Tm) and oligonucleotide stability calculations utilize unified nearest-neighbor thermodynamics (SantaLucia, 1998) with salt-correction parameters for magnesium and monovalent ions (Owczarzy et al., 2008). Nussinov dynamic programming folding tracebacks (Nussinov & Jacobson, 1980) calculate target minimum free energy (MFE) secondary structures. Additional features capture sequence-specific characteristics (GC content, primer length, GC clamps, 3'-dinucleotide stability, and off-target specificity mapping).

The model architecture consists of a stacked LightGBM ensemble of five Gradient Boosted Decision Tree (GBDT) classifiers combined with a Deep Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) sequence model. To produce reliable confidence scores, we apply Platt calibration to scale raw model logits into calibrated probability estimates representing the likelihood of amplification success. For scaled multiplex designs, panel selection is formulated as an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) problem, optimizing coverage while avoiding cross-primer dimerization using binary decision variables.

To adapt the model to specific laboratory enzymes and cycling protocols, PrimerForge supports lab-adaptive fine-tuning. This adaptation process is regularized using Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). EWC calculates the Fisher information matrix of the network parameters on general thermodynamic datasets to regularize the fine-tuning loss, preventing catastrophic forgetting of fundamental biophysical principles during local dataset adaptation.

Performance was evaluated using a stratified 80/20 train-test split (59-sample held-out test set). Model classification accuracy was measured via the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC-AUC) curve, and confidence calibration was assessed via the Brier score.
